Solve Everything

A blueprint arguing that artificial intelligence is about to turn thinking into a cheap utility — and that whether this delivers abundance or stagnation by 2035 depends on the institutions we build in the next 18 months.

Original essay by Dr Alexander D. Wissner-Gross & Dr Peter H. Diamandis

The whole argument in five sentences

AI is making intelligence so cheap that thinking becomes a utility, like electricity or tap water. Once that happens, many of humanity's biggest problems stop being about genius and become about logistics — you solve them by pouring in enough computing power. But abundance won't happen by accident; the authors call it "an engineering result." Whether we get a world of abundance or a "super-intelligent DMV" depends on the rules, targets and payment systems we lock in over roughly the next 18 months. The core instruction: stop paying for effort, start paying for verified results, set clear public targets, and aim AI at positive-sum "moonshots".

1Thinking is becoming a commodity

The economy used to pay for human effort — hours worked, reports written. The authors argue that economy is "dead".

Three things are happening at once between roughly 2026 and 2030: AI models are getting better than human experts, the cost of running them is collapsing towards the cost of the electricity they consume, and the friction of plugging them into real workflows is falling to almost nothing. When thinking is that cheap and that good, human brainpower is no longer the bottleneck.

The new bottleneck is simply aiming — deciding where to point this "firehose of intelligence". The new business question becomes "how much value did we create per pound of computing spent?" rather than "how many hours did we bill?"

2What "solved" actually means

A field is "solved", in the authors' sense, when the limiting factor stops being human cleverness and becomes the amount of computing power available. At that point you get a reliable result just by adding more compute — the way you get more light just by drawing more electricity.

Their vivid examples: feed in a mathematical conjecture and get a verified proof back in hours; sequence a brand-new virus and get a candidate cure within a day. The "miracle" becomes a predictable, repeatable engineering output rather than a stroke of genius.

Just as you don't marvel at the miracle of electricity every time you flip a light switch, you will not marvel at AI curing a disease or balancing the power grid. It will simply work, reliably and cheaply, in the background.

The authors frame each great revolution as a "war" on one scarce thing, and argue every one passes through the same four stages. Progress isn't magic — it's a sequence.

From hero to harness-builder

A recurring theme: prestige shifts from the lone genius (the "hero") to the person who builds the system that lets everyone solve that kind of problem (the "harness-builder").

Stop trying to be the hero who solves one problem. Build the harness that allows everyone to solve that class of problem.

4The maturity ladder: from "muddle" to "solved"

The authors describe a six-rung ladder that any field climbs as it becomes industrialised. At the bottom is "the muddle" — a problem so ill-defined you can't even measure success. At the top, the problem is "solved" and effectively free.

Their headline example is AlphaFold, which predicts protein structures. Work that once took a PhD student a year now takes minutes and costs pennies. The authors argue this wasn't luck: it happened because every layer was in place — a clear goal, good data (the Protein Data Bank), and a rigorous public competition (CASP) to score progress. The lesson: "Automate the evaluation before you automate the work." If you can't grade the test, you're only rehearsing.

5Targets pull the future towards them

A central mechanism: benchmarks should stop being passive scoreboards and become active "targeting systems" that direct money and effort. Set a clear, hard-to-cheat target with prize money attached, and capital — then capability — follows.

Where the target is set, capital follows. And where capital follows, capabilities compound.

A good target has three properties: it's legible (clearly measurable), adversarial (hard to game or cheat), and payable (tied to actual money). The advice to governments is blunt: stop writing detailed rules about how things should be done; instead define the metric, lock the prize money in escrow, and get out of the way.

The abundance flywheel

Once targets are set, the authors describe a self-reinforcing cycle that spins faster over time.

6When things get solved: the wavefront to 2035

The authors expect problems to fall in a predictable order — the cleanest, most digital problems first, then the physical world, then planetary-scale systems.

This is also why one solved field unlocks the next, like dominoes: solving maths helps solve physics, which helps solve materials science, which helps unlock practical fusion energy.

The "moonshots"

The essay sets out 15 concrete moonshots — ambitious, measurable goals — grouped into four families: human needs, the frontier of the mind, the planetary substrate, and the frontier of physics. Examples include growing replacement organs on demand, roughly doubling healthy human lifespan, ending hunger with synthetic food systems, giving every person a world-class AI tutor, and high-bandwidth brain–computer interfaces.

To qualify, a moonshot must be positive-sum (it grows the pie rather than just shifting it), auditable (you can verify it really worked), and composable (it builds on and feeds other advances).

7The fork in the road — and the deadline

The most urgent part of the argument: abundance is not guaranteed. The authors describe a closing window — roughly 18 months — in which the rules and norms around AI are still "molten" and can be shaped. Once they harden, they lock in for decades.

If we hard-code "bureaucracy" into the AI, we get a super-intelligent DMV. If we hard-code "outcomes," we get the Star Trek economy.

They lay out three possible futures:

Best case

The Bright Path

We build the right "rails", aim AI at moonshots, and reach genuine abundance.

Drift

The Muddle Path

Bureaucracy and old habits waste superintelligence on trivial things like optimising ad clicks. Stagnation.

Worst case

The Dark Path

A safety incident triggers panic and a regulatory "freeze" that halts progress.

"The Muddle" — entrenched bureaucracy, paying for effort instead of results, scarcity-minded institutions — is cast as the real enemy, more than any technical limit.

8The playbook: build the rails

The practical prescription is consistent throughout, aimed at anyone in a position to fund or direct work:

  • Publish the targets, then the budgets. Fund only verified, independently-checked results.
  • Stand up "action networks". Shared robotic labs and microfactories that are paid against outcome guarantees.
  • Escrow the compute. Pre-commit computing credits that only unlock when a benchmark is genuinely beaten.
  • Pay for results. Retire every contract that buys hours, reports or effort.
  • Instrument for trust. Wire automatic audit logs and safety "brakes" into the system itself.

The investment thesis follows from this: don't buy the AI models — they're becoming commodities that depreciate to zero. Buy the "rails" — the targeting systems, audit trails and payment infrastructure. These, the authors say, are the railroads of the 21st century.

9Safety and fairness are built into the engine

Safety isn't treated as a separate step that slows things down. Instead it's "mechanical": automatic systems that throttle or shut off the AI the instant its reliability, bias or fairness scores get worse. The slogan is "install better brakes and steering so we can drive faster, safely" — shaping speed rather than stopping it.

Fairness is treated as a pass/fail condition, not a nice-to-have:

A medical AI that cures cancer in 99% of patients but fails for 100% of a specific minority group is marked as a failure. Fairness is not an afterthought; it is a prerequisite for victory.

10Answers to the obvious objections

"This is just hype."

Every past revolution looked like hype until the measurement and payment layers clicked into place.

"Automation erases human value."

Automation commoditises the means, but humans still choose the ends — what to aim for.

"We must slow everything down."

The answer isn't to stop the car, but to fit better brakes and steering so it can go faster safely.

"Won't it just be luxury for the few?"

Abundance here means more ways to learn, heal and build per unit of energy — possibilities, not luxury.

So what should you take away?

Intelligence is becoming cheap and abundant, which turns many "hard" problems into questions of how you organise and aim resources. The technology is treated as close to inevitable; the outcome is not. The decisive variable is institutional — the targets we set, what we choose to pay for, and the guardrails we build — and the authors believe the window to set those defaults is closing fast. Their one-line call to action: pick a problem worth solving, define a clear measurable target, pay for the result rather than the effort, and help "lay the rails".

A quick glossary of the essay's invented terms

The Muddle
Entrenched bureaucracy and scarcity thinking — the main obstacle to abundance.
The Rails
The institutional infrastructure (targets, audits, payment systems) that channels AI towards good outcomes.
RoCS — Return on Cognitive Spend
Value created per pound of computing power; the proposed new core business metric.
Targeting Authority
A body that defines a metric and locks up prize money, instead of writing detailed rules.
Compute Escrow
Computing credits pre-committed and released only when a benchmark is genuinely beaten.
Action Network
Shared robotic labs / microfactories paid against guaranteed outcomes.
Domain Collapse
When a whole field drops from artisanal effort to cheap, instant, automated output (as AlphaFold did for protein structures).
The Shaped Charge
The image of focusing AI's power precisely at a chosen target rather than letting it disperse.
