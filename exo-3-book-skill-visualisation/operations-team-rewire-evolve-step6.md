## 3. Step 6 Principle

Step 6 is not "add more AI tools."

It is the organizational rewrite: replace informal coordination, role-based routing, and meeting-heavy event management with a small intelligence network where humans own judgment and agents handle structured coordination.

For the Operations Team, that means:

- The event workflow becomes the organizing unit.
- The Intelligence Stack becomes the operating rhythm.
- Every competition creates reusable learning.
- The company does not expand autonomy until governance keeps up.

## 4. Target Operating Model

### From Role-Centric To Pod-Centric

| Old pattern | Step 6 pattern |
|---|---|
| One person remembers how events are planned | Shared event intelligence workspace |
| Planning happens through meetings and messages | Planning happens through structured briefs, gates, queues, and logs |
| Lessons are reconstructed after the event | Lessons are captured during planning, event control, and post-event reporting |
| AI use depends on senior leadership | Every team member uses shared templates, agents, and review queues |
| Approval gates are implicit | Approval gates are explicit and logged |

### the Operations Team Intelligence Pods

the Operations Team is only 8 people, so "pods" should be light. A pod is a temporary operating cell around a workflow, not a new department.

| Pod | Purpose | Human owner | Agent support |
|---|---|---|---|
| Event Delivery Pod | Plan and run Track & Field competitions | Event Lead / Competition Director | Competition Planning Copilot |
| Results and Reporting Pod | Verify, summarize, and learn from event outcomes | Results Lead | Post-Event Reporting Copilot |
| Safety and Trust Pod | Protect athlete welfare, safeguarding, medical, and public trust gates | Safety / Safeguarding Lead | Gate checklists and escalation prompts only |
| Communications Pod | Draft coach/team/parent/public updates | Communications Lead | Drafting and tone support only |
| Learning and Governance Pod | Maintain evals, logs, rollback, HIDO, review queue, and vNext backlog | AI Operating Owner / Data Steward | Audit and review support |

## 6. Continuous Kill Switch

The Continuous Kill Switch is not only an emergency stop. It is the permanent operating loop for deciding what to stop, fix, migrate, or leave human-owned.

### Detection

| Signal | Trigger |
|---|---|
| Human override rate rises | Review agent spec, data quality, and eval coverage |
| Evals fail | Freeze affected workflow version |
| Forbidden action attempted | Stop workflow expansion and review permission envelope |
| Start-gate miss | Return to human-only gate for affected area |
| Sensitive data appears in draft | Remove output, escalate to Data Steward, update eval |
| Human review queue stalls | Reduce scope or name new approver |

### Action

| Trigger class | Action |
|---|---|
| Data quality issue | Fix source fields or block affected output |
| Agent behavior issue | Roll back prompt/template/rules version |
| Governance issue | Lower autonomy or stop real-data use |
| Human adoption issue | Add training or simplify workflow |
| Workflow success | Standardize asset and choose next candidate |

### Migration

| Decision | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Standardize | Make the workflow the default operating template |
| Iterate | Keep in shadow mode and improve vNext |
| Hold | Do not expand until governance catches up |
| Revert | Return to previous human-owned process |
| Retire | Stop low-value workflow or output |

## 7. Firm Boundary Redesign

the Operations Team should not treat every need as a hiring need. It should choose between internal humans, external specialists, partners, volunteers, suppliers, and agents based on trust, judgment, and repeatability.

| Capability | Best boundary choice | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Competition purpose and athlete experience | Internal human core | High trust and judgment |
| Safety, safeguarding, medical decisions | Internal named human or approved professional | High consequence |
| Results certification | Internal Results Lead / Technical Lead | Source-of-truth accountability |
| Timetable draft generation | Agent-assisted internal workflow | Repeatable structured task |
| Venue readiness checks | Internal owner plus venue partner | Shared physical accountability |
| Communications drafts | Agent-assisted, human-approved | Reputation-sensitive |
| Specialist technical officiating | External / partner network | Event-specific capability |
| Post-event report drafts | Agent-assisted internal workflow | Strong LEARN-layer value |

## 8. Organizational Half-Life

the Operations Team should measure how quickly its operating model improves.

| Measure | Starting point | Step 6 target |
|---|---|---|
| Planning template half-life | Ad hoc / event-specific | Reviewed after every event |
| Role-card half-life | New v0.2 asset | Updated quarterly or after incidents |
| Timetable rules half-life | v0.2/v0.3 | Updated after each event with drift data |
| Eval suite half-life | Synthetic only | Updated after every real event |
| Governance half-life | Designed, not live | Reviewed fortnightly during sprint |

**Board-level question:** How long before half of the Operations Team's event operating method is obsolete?

Initial answer: likely 6-12 months. Step 6 target: shorten the learning cycle without destabilizing safety, trust, or team adoption.

## 9. Compensation And Recognition Link

For a small company, this should be lightweight. Do not create bureaucracy.

Recognize and reward:

- Turning tacit knowledge into reusable templates.
- Improving evals after real failures or near-misses.
- Teaching another team member how to use the workflow.
- Reducing coordination load without reducing human accountability.
- Catching a governance issue before it reaches athletes, coaches, or the public.

Do not reward:

- More autonomous AI use for its own sake.
- Faster publishing without approval.
- Bypassing review queues.
- Unlogged agent recommendations.
