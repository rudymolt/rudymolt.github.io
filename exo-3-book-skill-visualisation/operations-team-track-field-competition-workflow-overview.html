ExO REWRITE steps
REWRITE is the ExO change process. It takes an organization from "we use AI tools sometimes" to "we have a repeatable AI-supported operating system." The steps move from big-picture direction to practical workflow proof, then finally to a new operating rhythm.

Competition loop

The workflow is designed to learn from every event.

The key shift is from one-off event planning to a repeatable loop. Each event generates structured evidence that improves the next brief, rules module, role cards, safety tests, and governance controls.

Draft-only is a design choice.The workflow stays draft-only because safety, results, eligibility, spend, and communications remain human-owned.
Shadow mode creates the learning data.The valuable signal is where human decisions diverge from copilot recommendations and why.
Governance is part of the workflow.Safety tests, source tracking, a return-to-previous-version plan, and human review lists are not later compliance tasks. They are part of how the workflow runs.
The new planning rhythm needs proof.After one real event proves the workflow works, the Operations Team turns it into its normal competition-planning routine: small working groups, clear owners, and a routine for stopping, fixing, or returning to the previous approved version if anything is unsafe.
New planning rhythm

Turn the proven event workflow into the Operations Team's normal competition routine.

This is the final adoption stage of the REWRITE playbook. It does not mean giving the copilot more power. It means that once a real event proves the workflow is useful and safe, the Operations Team plans, reviews, reports, and improves every future event through the same repeatable system.

What changes for the team? Event planning stops being a one-off scramble. The same brief, review list, reports, and improvement log are used every time.
Who still decides? People still approve safety, timetable, spending, results, and communications. The copilot helps prepare the decision, but does not own it.
What is the stop/fix/return routine? Plainly: if the workflow gives unsafe or low-quality output, the Operations Team pauses it, fixes it, or returns to the previous approved version.
When is the new planning rhythm real? Only after a live competition proves the process saves time, protects trust, and produces better learning for the next event.
What the test pilot showed

The Track & Field planning test is promising, but not ready for automation.

We ran a worked example with the Operations Team planning a Track & Field competition. The copilot helped draft the plan, timetable, review checks, communications, and post-event report. The results support keeping the workflow draft-only and preparing for a real shadow pilot. They do not support autonomous execution.

43/80Overall readiness: enough AI usage to start, but stronger foundations are needed before scaling.
Level 2 of 5Safety controls maturity: the four controls exist in draft form, but need stronger logs, return plans, and review routines.
14%Planning edit rate: humans changed or rejected 14% of copilot suggestions in the example competition run.
50%Reporting workflow v0.1: half of the post-event reporting suggestions needed human changes, so this workflow needs another version.
Decision: continue with the planning copilot in draft-only mode. Do not automate real competition decisions yet. Next, run one actual Track & Field event in shadow mode and compare the copilot's suggestions with human decisions.

Plan the event A draft-only planning copilot that can prepare timetables, checks, role cards, and communications. Test it beside humans on one real Track & Field event.
